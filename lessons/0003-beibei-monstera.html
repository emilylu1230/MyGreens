About Beibei

Monstera deliciosa is one of the most rewarding indoor plants — it grows fast, the leaves get bigger over time, and the iconic splits (fenestrations) develop as the plant matures. You have cuttings propagating in a two-chamber self-watering pot, and a moss pole in progress — both excellent signs you know what you're doing.

The name 龟背竹 means "turtle-back bamboo" — a reference to the split pattern on mature leaves resembling a turtle shell.

Light

Beibei needs bright indirect light. Place near your bedroom window but out of direct sun. The big leaves are highly efficient light collectors — this is actually one of the easier plants to keep happy in a bedroom with a large window.

Rotate 90° weekly (which you're already doing — keep it up). This prevents the plant leaning heavily toward the light and ensures all sides develop evenly.

No splits on new leaves? Young Monstera leaves come out whole with no holes. Splits and holes (fenestrations) develop from about the 4th–5th leaf onward, and only if the plant is getting enough light. If new large leaves are still coming out whole, move it slightly closer to the window.

Watering

Let the top 5 cm of soil dry out before watering. Monstera is more drought-tolerant than most people think — it's far more commonly killed by overwatering than underwatering.

In winter (now), reduce watering to every 10–14 days. The self-watering pot for the cuttings is fine as-is — just don't top up the reservoir too frequently in winter.

Aerial roots Monstera grows thick aerial roots from its stems. These are normal and healthy — don't cut them off. Guide them toward the moss pole or into the soil. They absorb moisture and anchor the plant as it climbs.

The moss pole

Monstera naturally climbs trees in the wild — a moss pole mimics this and encourages larger, more split leaves. Keep the moss pole moist (spray with water a few times a week) so the aerial roots attach to it. Once roots grab the pole, new leaves above that point will be noticeably larger.

Moving outside (Spring)

From September, Beibei can go outside in a shaded spot — under an eave or filtered through a shade cloth. This is ideal: it will grow much faster outdoors. Avoid direct sun (leaves burn) and bring inside if the temperature goes above 38°C or if there's strong wind.

Common problems

Yellow leaves
Overwatering — the most common cause.
Let soil dry more between waterings. Check roots aren't sitting in water.
Brown, crispy leaf tips
Low humidity or tap water fluoride.
Use filtered water if possible. Increase humidity with a pebble tray.
Small new leaves with no splits
Not enough light, or the plant needs a bigger pot/more nutrients.
Move closer to window. Fertilise in spring once growth resumes.
Drooping, wilting leaves
Underwatering, or heat stress.
Water thoroughly. On very hot Adelaide days, move away from window.

Seasonal care — Adelaide

  • Winter (now – Aug): Reduce watering. No fertiliser. Growth slows — don't panic.
  • Spring (Sept): Move outside to shaded spot if possible. Resume fertilising. Growth surges.
  • Summer (Nov–Mar): Outside is great — just protect from direct midday sun and extreme heat days.
