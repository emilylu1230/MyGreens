About Spider

Spider plants are one of the easiest indoor plants in existence — they tolerate neglect, low light, irregular watering, and even being pot-bound. They're also pet-safe, which is rare among popular houseplants.

The name comes from the long trailing stems (stolons) that produce baby plants — "spiderettes" — which dangle like spiders on a web. You have it in the study room, which is a great spot.

Light

The darker study room suits Spider well. It thrives in low to medium indirect light and will tolerate quite dim conditions that would stress most other plants. It can handle bright indirect light too, but direct sun bleaches the leaves.

If leaves are very pale or yellow, it may need slightly more light — move it closer to a window or to a brighter room.

Watering

Water when the top 3–4 cm of soil is dry. Spider plants have thick, fleshy roots that store water — they handle drought well. In Adelaide winter, water every 10–14 days at most.

Brown tips — it's the tap water Spider plants are notably sensitive to fluoride and chlorine in Adelaide tap water. Brown, crispy leaf tips (not connected to underwatering) almost always signal this. Use filtered water, collected rainwater, or leave tap water to sit overnight before using.

Spiderettes — signs of a happy plant

When a spider plant is healthy and getting enough light, it produces long arching stems with small plantlets at the tips. These are a clear sign the plant is thriving. You can:

  • Leave them on — they look great cascading from a hanging pot
  • Propagate — pin a spiderette into moist soil while still attached; once it roots (2–3 weeks), cut the stem. You can also simply cut and place in water.

Common problems

Brown, crispy tips
Fluoride/chlorine in tap water — very common.
Switch to filtered water or rainwater. Trim brown tips with clean scissors cut at an angle.
Pale, washed-out leaves
Too much direct light, or not enough light (variegation fades).
If in direct sun, move to indirect. If very dim, move to a brighter spot.
No spiderettes
Not enough light, or plant is too young / recently repotted.
Move to slightly brighter indirect spot. Spiderettes come when the plant is comfortable and established.
Mushy crown or base
Overwatering or water sitting at the base of leaves.
Let dry out completely. Improve drainage. Water at soil level, not into the crown.

Seasonal care — Adelaide

  • Winter: Very hardy. Reduce watering. Can tolerate cooler temps down to about 10°C.
  • Spring/Summer: Growth picks up. A good time to propagate spiderettes.
  • Outdoors: Can go outside in a shaded spot — avoid direct sun.
