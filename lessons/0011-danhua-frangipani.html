About 蛋花

Frangipani (Plumeria) is a tropical and subtropical tree famous for its intensely fragrant waxy flowers — white, yellow, pink, or red depending on variety. In Adelaide's warm climate it can be grown outdoors year-round with winter dormancy.

Unlike every other plant in your collection, frangipani is deciduous in winter — it loses its leaves, looks completely bare and dead, and then pushes new growth from branch tips in September. This is entirely normal. Don't throw it out.

While it's still indoors (now — Aug)

Since it's currently winter and 蛋花 is inside, keep it as follows:

  • Put it in the sunniest spot possible — the brightest window, no curtain blocking light
  • Water very sparingly — once every 3–4 weeks at most. In winter dormancy it barely needs any water. Overwatering in dormancy is the main cause of root rot in frangipani.
  • It may already be losing leaves or look bare — this is normal winter dormancy
  • No fertiliser until it's outside and actively growing

Moving outside — September

From September, once nights are reliably above 15°C, move 蛋花 outside to the sunniest spot in your garden. It needs at least 6 hours of direct sun per day to flower. Under an eave or in a pot on a sunny patio is ideal.

Adelaide's summers are perfect for frangipani — hot, dry weather with full sun is exactly what they want. Water more regularly once it's actively growing and flowering (every 1–2 weeks), but never let it sit in waterlogged soil.

Annual cycle in Adelaide

Jun – Aug
Dormancy. Bare branches, no leaves. Keep indoors or sheltered. Water once every 3–4 weeks only.
September
Move outside to full sun. New growth appears at branch tips. Start watering more regularly.
Oct – Nov
Active growth. Begin fertilising monthly with a high-potassium fertiliser (encourages flowers). Water weekly.
Dec – Mar
Peak flowering season. Full sun, regular water. Enjoy the flowers and fragrance.
Apr – May
Growth slows. Reduce watering. Leaves begin to yellow and drop — prepare for dormancy.

Common problems

No flowers
Not enough direct sun, or too much nitrogen fertiliser (promotes leaves not flowers).
Move to a sunnier spot. Use a fertiliser high in phosphorus/potassium (e.g. tomato fertiliser) rather than general purpose.
Soft, mushy stem base
Root or stem rot — almost always from overwatering in winter dormancy.
Cut away the rotten section with a clean blade to healthy white/cream tissue. Let the cut dry for a week before placing in dry soil. Don't water again for 2–3 weeks.
Leaves dropping in autumn
Normal dormancy — not a problem.
Nothing. This is supposed to happen.
Rust-coloured spots on leaves
Frangipani rust fungus — common in humid conditions.
Remove affected leaves. Improve airflow. Treat with a copper-based fungicide if severe.
