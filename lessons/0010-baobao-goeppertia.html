About 豹豹

Goeppertia concinna (formerly Calathea lancifolia) is the rattlesnake plant — named for the alternating dark oval markings on long, wavy lance-shaped leaves. The undersides are purple-red, one of the most distinctive features. Like 雀雀, it's a prayer plant — leaves rise at night and open in the morning.

The health of 豹豹's markings and leaf colour is a direct indicator of care quality: rich purple-red undersides and bold dark spots = healthy. Faded, pale, or dull = needs better humidity, light, or water quality.

Light

Medium indirect only. No direct sun — it causes bleaching and curling. Unlike 皮皮 which needs maximum light, 豹豹 is more comfortable slightly back from the window. The long leaves evolved to catch dappled light in the undergrowth of tropical forests.

Watering

Keep soil consistently lightly moist — the same approach as 雀雀. The top centimetre can dry, but below that should remain slightly moist. Never let it fully dry out, and never let it sit in waterlogged soil.

Use filtered water or rainwater — tap water causes brown edges within weeks.

Reading the leaves If leaves curl inward lengthwise: the plant is too dry or too cold. If leaves are yellowing: likely overwatering or cold. If the purple-red on the undersides fades: humidity is too low. These are your three main signals.

Group 豹豹 and 雀雀 together

Both Goeppertia/Calathea plants have the same humidity needs. Placing them near each other creates a localised higher-humidity zone through transpiration (plants release moisture). Add a pebble tray under both, and you've built a simple humidity microclimate without a humidifier.

Common problems

Brown, crispy leaf edges
Low humidity — almost always this in Adelaide.
Pebble tray, group with 雀雀, consider a small humidifier.
Curling leaves
Too dry (soil or air) or too cold.
Check soil moisture. Check temperature — keep above 16°C. Raise humidity.
Fading markings / pale undersides
Extended low humidity or insufficient light.
Improve humidity. Move to slightly brighter (but still indirect) spot.
Yellow leaves
Overwatering or cold.
Let soil dry slightly more between waterings. Keep away from cold windows in winter.
Spider mites (fine webbing on leaves)
Very low humidity — spider mites thrive in dry conditions.
Wipe leaves with a damp cloth. Increase humidity. Treat with neem oil if infestation persists.

Seasonal care — Adelaide

  • Winter (now — highest risk period): Humidity management is critical. Keep above 18°C. No fertiliser. Filtered water only. Don't repot.
  • Spring/Summer: Growth resumes. Fertilise monthly with diluted liquid fertiliser. Can benefit from humidity of a sheltered shaded outdoor spot on mild days.
  • Never in direct Adelaide sun or heat.
