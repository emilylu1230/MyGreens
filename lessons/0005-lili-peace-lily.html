Why Lili is beginner-friendly

Peace lilies are one of the most forgiving indoor plants. They communicate clearly — when they need water, leaves droop noticeably. When light is too strong, leaves yellow. When they're happy, they produce elegant white flowers.

Lili is also one of the best air-purifying plants you can have indoors, according to the classic NASA Clean Air Study.

Light

Lili is the most shade-tolerant plant in your collection. She's happy well away from the window, or in a spot that only gets low ambient light. Avoid any direct sun — even through a sheer curtain, strong Adelaide sun will burn her leaves.

If she's flowering, give her slightly more light (medium indirect) to encourage blooms. If flowers aren't a priority, she's perfectly content in the darker corner of your room.

Watering — let Lili tell you

Peace lilies have a built-in watering signal: when they need water, leaves droop slightly — not collapsed, just a gentle sag. This is healthy and normal. Water her at this point and she'll perk up within hours.

Don't wait until leaves are completely limp — that's more stress than needed. But also don't water on a fixed schedule. Let her droop slightly, then water. This works better than any calendar.

Overwatering is the main risk Peace lilies are killed by overwatering more often than anything else. The soil should dry out partially between waterings. If soil is constantly wet, roots rot. After the recent repot, be especially conservative — water only when Lili signals she needs it.
Brown tips — tap water issue Peace lilies are sensitive to chlorine and fluoride in tap water. If brown tips appear despite correct watering and light, switch to filtered water or collected rainwater.

Flowering

Peace lilies flower when they're slightly stressed — typically in spring or after a period of lower light. The white "flower" is actually a spathe (a modified leaf) surrounding a small spike. To encourage flowering:

  • Move to slightly brighter indirect light in September
  • Resume fertilising with a balanced fertiliser once per month in spring/summer
  • Don't overwater

Common problems

Drooping leaves
Needs water — this is normal signalling behaviour.
Water thoroughly. Should perk up within a few hours.
Yellow leaves
Too much light, overwatering, or natural ageing of lower leaves.
Move further from window. Let soil dry more between waterings. Trim yellow leaves at the base.
Brown leaf tips
Fluoride/chlorine in tap water, or low humidity.
Use filtered water. Keep away from air conditioning.
Black or brown patches on leaves
Direct sunburn.
Move well away from direct light. Damaged leaves won't recover — trim them.

Seasonal care — Adelaide

  • Winter (now): Reduce watering. Keep above 15°C. No fertiliser until September.
  • Spring: Resume fertilising monthly. Can move to brighter spot to encourage flowers.
  • Summer: Keep indoors out of direct sun. Will droop in high heat — this is heat stress, not thirst. Move away from the window on 40°C+ days.
