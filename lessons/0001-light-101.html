Lesson 1 of your plant journey

Light 101: Reading Your Room

The single most important thing you can know about keeping your plants alive — before water, soil, or fertiliser.

Why light comes first

Most indoor plants die from wrong light, not bad luck. Too much direct sun scorches leaves. Too little and the plant slowly starves — it can't photosynthesise enough energy to stay alive. Get light right and everything else becomes much easier.

Your master bedroom in Adelaide is actually a great setup — a large openable window, curtains you can adjust, and an outdoor space to move plants into. Once you understand what each light level means, you'll know exactly where each plant belongs.

The four light levels

☀️

Direct sun

Sunlight hits the leaf surface directly. Hot and intense — most indoor tropicals hate this.

🌤

Bright indirect

Very well-lit room. No direct rays on leaves. Near a window but behind glass or curtain sheer.

⛅

Medium

Comfortable reading light. Away from the window, or window is partly blocked.

🌥

Low

You'd need a lamp to read comfortably. Deep in a room or a south-facing window.

Quick test Hold your hand about 30 cm above a white piece of paper at midday. Sharp, hard shadow = bright indirect or direct. Soft, blurry shadow = medium. Barely any shadow = low.

Your window in Adelaide — direction matters

Australia is in the Southern Hemisphere, so the sun arcs across the northern sky. This is the opposite of what most plant advice assumes (which is written for Europe or North America). Use this guide for your master bedroom window:

Which way does your window face?

North-facing Bright all day. Best spot in the house. Warm in winter, very hot in Adelaide summer — use your curtain sheer in Jan–Feb.
South-facing Low light all year. No direct sun. Only peace lily and very tolerant plants will be happy here long-term.
East-facing Gentle morning sun, shaded afternoon. Excellent for bright-indirect plants. Safer than west in Adelaide heat.
West-facing Harsh afternoon sun — brutal in an Adelaide summer. Keep curtains closed or move heat-sensitive plants in November–March.
Adelaide summer alert On 40°C+ days, direct sun through glass acts like a magnifying glass and can burn leaves even on plants that normally like bright light. On extreme heat days, keep curtains closed and don't open the window if the outside air is hotter than inside.

The humidity problem 雀雀 (Calathea orbifolia) and 豹豹 (Goeppertia concinna) both need ≥60% humidity. Adelaide's winter air is very dry. This is the hardest thing to manage in your collection — we'll cover it in a dedicated lesson. For now: keep them away from heating vents and avoid misting (it causes fungal spots). A pebble tray with water under the pot is a safer first step.

Practice: map your room

Before the next lesson, do this one thing:

  1. Find out which direction your window faces. Use the compass on your phone, or check Google Maps with your house. Write it down.
  2. Do the shadow test at midday in the spot each plant currently sits.
  3. Check the table above — is each plant getting the right level?

If a plant is in the wrong spot, that's the first thing to fix. Light problems cause 90% of the symptoms people think are watering problems.
